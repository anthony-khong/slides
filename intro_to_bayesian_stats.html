Inference using Bayes Theorem

$$y_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$$

How do we infer $(\mu, \sigma^2)$ from $\{y_i\}_{i=1}^N$?

Frequentist Solution

$$\hat{\mu} = \dfrac{1}{N}\sum_i Y_i$$ $$\hat{\sigma}^2 = \dfrac{1}{N}\sum_i (Y_i - \hat{\mu})^2$$

Big Idea:

Find estimators whose sampling distributions are lose to the unknown true parameters.

Bayesian Solution

$$ \pi(\mu, \sigma^2 | \boldsymbol y = \dfrac{p(\boldsymbol y | \mu, \sigma^2) \pi(\mu, \sigma^2)}{p(\boldsymbol y)} $$

Big Idea:

Formulate a posterior belief of the unknown parameters by combining our prior beliefs with our data.

The Bayesian Recipe

$\boldsymbol\theta$: Parameter of Interest

$\mathcal{D}$: Data

$\pi(\boldsymbol \theta)$: Prior

$\p(\mathcal{D} | \boldsymbol \theta)$: Likelihood

$\pi(\boldsymbol \theta | \mathcal{D})$: Posterior

Bayes Theorem:

$\pi(\boldsymbol \theta | \mathcal{D}) \propto p(\mathcal{D} | \boldsymbol \theta) \pi(\boldsymbol\theta)
